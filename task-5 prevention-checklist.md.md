PART-B Prevention Checklist

**01. Use a unique, strong password for every account**

Never reuse the same password across platforms. A breach on one site
instantly compromises all accounts using the same credentials. Use a
passphrase like *VIT@Bhopal#2025* --- long, memorable, and hard to
crack.

*Action: Set up a free password manager (Bitwarden) to store credentials
securely.*

**02. Enable two-factor authentication (2FA) on all important accounts**

Activate 2FA on your email, social media, and banking apps. Even if your
password is stolen, the attacker cannot access your account without the
second factor (OTP or authenticator app code).

*Action: Enable 2FA on Gmail, Instagram, and LinkedIn immediately ---
takes under 2 minutes each.*

**03. Log out of shared or public devices completely**

On college lab computers, library systems, or a friend\'s phone, always
log out after use. Saving passwords or staying signed in on shared
devices gives anyone who uses that device next full access to your
accounts.

*Action: Use browser incognito/private mode on any device that is not
your own.*

**04. Understand collect requests --- approving always means sending
money**

UPI-specific

A UPI \"Collect Request\" debits your account --- it does not credit it.
Fraudsters disguise collection requests as \"receive money\" prompts. If
someone asks you to \"approve a request to receive your
scholarship/refund/prize,\" that is a scam. You never need to enter your
PIN to receive money on UPI.

*Rule to remember: PIN = sending money. No PIN is ever needed to receive
funds.*

**05. Never share your OTP, UPI PIN, or CVV with anyone --- including
bank officials**

UPI-specific

Your bank, NPCI, or any legitimate organisation will never call and ask
for your OTP or UPI PIN. These are private, one-way credentials. The
moment you share them, the transaction is authorised and irreversible.
Disconnect any call asking for this information immediately.

*Action: If you receive such a call, hang up and report to your bank\'s
official helpline.*

**06. Verify links before clicking --- check the full URL carefully**

Phishing links mimic real websites using slight
variations: *gov.in.verify-kyc.com* is not a government site --- it is a
trap. Before clicking any link in a message or email, hover over it (on
desktop) or long-press (on mobile) to preview the actual URL. If
anything looks off, do not click.

*Action: Go directly to official websites by typing the address in the
browser rather than following links.*

**07. Be sceptical of urgent, too-good-to-be-true messages**

Fraudsters rely on urgency and excitement to suppress rational thinking.
Messages like \"You have won ₹50,000 --- claim in 24 hours\" or \"Your
KYC will expire today\" are engineered to make you act before you think.
Pause, verify through an official source, and never act under artificial
pressure.

*Action: Call the official helpline of the organisation being
impersonated to confirm any such claim.*

**08. Download apps only from official stores --- Google Play or Apple
App Store**

APK files shared via WhatsApp, Telegram, or unknown websites are a
primary vector for malware that can silently read your OTPs, access your
UPI apps, or monitor your screen. No legitimate bank, government portal,
or service will ask you to install an app via a link in a message.

*Action: Delete any APK files currently on your device and scan with
Google Play Protect.*

09\. Audit your social media privacy settings every semester

Public profiles expose your college, city, schedule, and social circle
to strangers --- information that is actively used by fraudsters to
build convincing, personalised scams. Set your posts to \"Friends
only,\" disable location tagging, and remove your phone number from
public visibility on all platforms.

*Action: Open Instagram/Facebook settings right now and switch to a
private account if you haven\'t already.*

**10. Keep your devices, apps, and browsers updated**

Software updates patch known security vulnerabilities. Cybercriminals
actively exploit outdated operating systems and apps --- especially
older versions of Android and browsers. Delaying updates is one of the
most common and easily avoidable reasons devices get compromised.
